Interstitial lung disease (ILD) imaging

Navigating ILD: a 4 step diagnostic approach

Zakaria Guennoun M.D

1

IS IT FIBROTIC LUNG DISEASE?

The Core Question: Is Scarring Present?

The Scarring Signature

  • Subpleural Reticulation (Net-like pattern)
  • Traction Bronchiectasis (Dilated airways due to surrounding fibrosis)
  • Volume Loss (Diminished lung size)
  • Architectural Distortion (Loss of normal lung structure)
  • Irregular Interlobular Septal Thickening

PATTERN PREDOMINANCE & DISTRIBUTION

Step 2: Distribution (Where is it?) → Step 3: Pattern (What does it look like?)

UIP (Usual Interstitial Pneumonia)

Distribution Key: Heterogeneous, Apicobasal Gradient, Peripheral/Subpleural

Predominant Finding: Honeycombing

NSIP (Nonspecific Interstitial Pneumonia)

Distribution Key: Homogeneous, Lower Lobes, Diffuse/Subpleural Sparing

Predominant Finding: Ground Glass Opacities

FHP (Fibrotic Hypersensitivity Pneumonitis)

Distribution Key: Heterogeneous, Middle/Lower Lobes, Central/Perihilar Predominance

Predominant Finding: Mosaic Attenuation (Air Trapping)

3

ANCILLARY FINDINGS & ETIOLOGY CLUES

Searching for Secondary Causes and Specific Markers

IPF / Probable UIP

Diagnosis of Exclusion: Unknown Cause

  • Requires UIP pattern or Probable UIP pattern.
  • Crucially requires the ABSENCE of Atypical Features: (Ground Glass Opacities, Air Trapping, Micronodules, Extensive Mosaic Attenuation)

Progressive Fibrosing ILD (PIF)

A designation for a progressive phenotype.

  • Applies ACROSS etiologies (e.g., PIF-HP, PIF-CTD).
  • Requires evidence of Fibrosis Progression over time.
  • Important for therapeutic decision-making.

CT-ILD (Connective Tissue ILD) Clues

When to suspect an underlying systemic disease.

  • Four Corner Sign: Fibrosis affecting all four pulmonary regions (upper, lower, anterior, posterior). Strongly associated with: Systemic Sclerosis (SSc) / Scleroderma.
  • Exuberant Honeycombing (HC): Honeycombing that is visually more extensive, cystic, or prominent than what is typical for IPF. Strongly associated with: Rheumatoid Arthritis (RA) and Systemic Sclerosis (SSc).
  • Straight-edge Fibrosis: The abrupt termination of reticular opacities (fibrosis) at the border of the subpleural region. Strongly associated with: Systemic Sclerosis (SSc).
  • Esophageal Dilatation: Abnormal widening of the esophagus due to smooth muscle atrophy, reflecting systemic involvement. Strongly associated with: Systemic Sclerosis (SSc).
  • Clavicular Erosions: Bone destruction or wearing away in the clavicle, often specific for chronic arthropathy. Highly specific for: Rheumatoid Arthritis (RA).

Other Etiology Markers

  • Asbestosis: Look for Calcified Pleural Plaques
  • Drug Induced: May show Hyperattenuating Hepatic Parenchyma (among other findings)
  • IgG4-Related Disease: Consider if there is Pancreas or other extrapulmonary organ involvement.

CLINICAL INTEGRATION & FINAL DIAGNOSIS

Integrating Imaging Clues with Patient Context

The Three Pillars of Clinical History

1. WHO? (Patient Demographics/Population)

Example: A young patient suggests Lymphangioleiomyomatosis (LAM), while an older smoker suggests IPF.

2. SMOKING STATUS? (Environmental Clue)

Heavy smoking history can point towards Respiratory Bronchiolitis-ILD (RB-ILD) or Desquamative Interstitial Pneumonia (DIP). Essential context for diagnosis of exclusion like IPF.

3. EXTRAPULMONARY SYMPTOMS? (CTD Red Flag)

Joint pain, skin rashes, Raynaud's phenomenon strongly suggest Connective Tissue Disorder (CT-ILD), overriding a typical UIP pattern on imaging alone.

THE FINAL DIAGNOSTIC ECHO

The ultimate diagnosis is reached by integrating the identified Pattern (UIP/NSIP/FHP) with the Ancillary Clues and the crucial Clinical History. Imaging is essential but is just one component of the multi-disciplinary team approach.
